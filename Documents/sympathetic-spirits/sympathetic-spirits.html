Sympathetic Spirits: An Examination of Spiritualistic Beliefs and Practices in the Development of American Religion

Introduction

The purpose of this project is to provide a sympathetic view of American spiritualistic beliefs and practices and to establish terms which will help distinguish different spiritulistic ideas. I aim to present different spiritualistic thinkers' ideas about and experiences with the spirit world so that outside observers can better understand how and why people develop these beliefs. I will also provide historical contextualization to elucidate how a diversity of spiritualistic beliefs and experiences developed. Belief in spirits and the spirit world has a deep history in American religion and understanding the history and development of spiritualistic beliefs and practices requires taking the perspectives of believers and practitioners seriously. Examination of these perspectives not only provides unique and inherently interesting cases to examine, but helps us to relate to and better understand people who many of us interact with on a daily basis.

What is a Spirit?

Throughout American history, spirits have generally been thought to be a form of human existence which operate in a realm seperate from matter. Emmanuel Swedenborg is often recognized as one of the first thinkers to propose the existence of a spirit world, which corresponds to life on earth. For instance, in his work Heaven and Hell, Swedenborg recorded his visions of an afterlife in which man and women continue to sleep, wake, eat, drink, and go about their daily life in an environment mirroring earthly existence. The notion that human beings continue exist in an environment seperate from, yet mirroring material existence after death is charateristic of spirit belief. According to the scholar Kenneth D. Pimple, the difference between belief and spirits and belief in ghosts is that ghosts have historically been tied to a specific physical location associated with their life and death. Spirits on the other hand, exist in a seperate environment, untethered from the material world.

What are "Spiritualistic" Beliefs and Practices?

Recently, scholars such as Catherine Albanese and Erik Seeman have begun to recognize certain religious beliefs and practices as "spiritualistic." People with spiritualistic inclinations tend to characterize the afterlife as largely reflective of life on earth and often attempt to interact with spirits. The following religions are characterized as spiritualistic beliefs and practices:

  • Swedenborgianism
  • Shakerism
  • Spiritualism
What is Spiritualism?

Spiritualism is a religion that is composed of a diversity of spiritualistic beliefs and practices which emerged in the United States in the mid-nineteenth century and which maintained prevelence up until the early twentieth century. Spiritualists never established a unified religious institution, but many individuals who identified as Spiritualists frequently corresponded and collaborated. What unified most Spiritualists is the belief in mediumship, or the idea that particular individuals had an increased sensitivity to the spirit world and were capable of communicating with, or facilitating communication with, spirits. Many scholars have turned away from characterizing Spiritualism as a unified movement and have instead turn toward notable individuals who were the first to establish spiritualistic beliefs and practices outside of existing religious groups, such as the Swedenborgians and Shakers. On this page, I will briefly discuss who many scholars take to be the "founders" of phenomenal and philosophical Spiritualism, the Fox Sisters and Andrew Jackson Davis.

Phenomenal Spiritualism

Many scholars have taken the Fox Sisters as the start of the Spiritualist "Movement," however I will introduce them as some of the first Spiritualist practitioners to popularize phenomenal Spiritualism. The term "phenomenal Spiritualism" refers to beliefs and practices which concerned themselves primarily with empirical demonstrations of the spirit world through séances, planchettes, and other observable phenomena. The Fox Sisters drew large crowds to their home in Hydesville, New York, by claiming to communicate with spirits through producing “spirit rappings.” These “rappings” were knocking noises without a detectable source which acted as a code in transmitting words and sentences from spirits who were asked questions by the sisters. The Fox sisters quickly became well-known in New York, with as many as five-hundred people visiting their home at one time in 1849. Following the Fox sisters’ spirit communications, Spiritualism quickly spread throughout New York, with the Spiritualist Register reporting as many as 240,000 registered Spiritualists in the state prior to the Civil War, totaling 6% of the state’s total population. The majority of Spiritualists seem to have been more interested in the phenomenal Spiritualism of the Fox sisters than philosophical Spiritualism. As Spiritualism spread through the United States, gatherings often revolved around the spectacles of psychical phenomena as seen through commodified devices such as the planchette and Ouija board. The commodification of phenomenal Spiritualism led skeptics and believers alike to associate Spiritualism with spectacle and entertainment. Below is a list of people associated with phenomenal Spiritualism and the popularization of Spiritualist spectacles:

  • The Fox Sisters
  • Mina Crandon
  • Arthur Ford
Philosophical Spiritualism

Recently, many scholars of Spiritualism, such as Sally Morita and Robert Delp, have distinguished philosophical Spiritualism from phenomenal Spiritualism. Philosophical Spiritualists tended to be more interested in visionary experiences, the cosmology of the spirit world, and social reform than phenomenal Spiritualists, who were more interested in reproducing empirical evidence of the spirit world. Andrew Jackson Davis was one of the first Spiritualists to make this distinction, and frequently criticized "phenomenal Spiritualists" for their skepticism and need to seek empirical evidence of the afterlife. In his Great Harmonia, Davis wrote:

Many investigators of Phenomenal Spiritualism may have been, and doubtlessly are, illogical in their thoughts; therefore, also, in their actions and character. The cold, repulsive skeptic of last year is our unbridled fanatic of to-day; simply because his mind is fevered with the blazing propensities of this immortal treasure of future existence.

Philosophical Spiritualists such as Davis were not overly concerned with phenomenal “proofs” of Spiritualism such as spirit photographs, ectoplasm, or spirit channeling because they did not believe that it was necessary to provide evidence of the afterlife. Instead. philosophical Spiritualists tended to focus on subjective visionary experiences and historic records of these experiences to inform cosmological and social models. However, philosophical and phenomological forms of Spiritualism were not mutually exclusive. For instance, the Spiritualist Arthur Conan Doyle recognized Davis’s philosophy as one of the most authoritative sources regarding the spirit world, while also defending and promoting psychical phenomena. Below are a list of some prominent Spiritualists who were particularly interested in philosophical Spiritualism:

  • Andrew Jackson Davis
  • Arthur Conan Doyle
  • Emma Hardinge Britten
Modern Relevance

Today, scholars such as Catherine Albanese and Erik Seeman reference other spiritualistic or "metaphysical" religions as deeply influenced by Spiritualist thinkers. Reformulations of Spiritualist thought made by the Theosophical Society, the New Thought movement, and Christian Science redirected the idea of a spirit existence across notions of occultism, mind cure, and a transformation of the Self. While the Spiritualism itself faded from popularity after the early nineteenth century, the same impulses that motivated its shortlived popularity went on to inform other religious movements. Understanding how modern spiritual beliefs and practices took shape---including divinatory practices, ideas of the afterlife and other worlds, and understandings of the self and its continuity---requires tracing these historical geneaology of these cultural developments and understanding the specific religious desires they fulfill.
